01 — The Insights

Four gaps in the JD.
All ops-intern-solvable.

Each maps to a specific JD line. None require engineering. All four can be prototyped in spreadsheets and Notion — exactly what this role is built to do.

01
Timezone-blind follow-ups manufacture false churn

The JD says "manage communications across multiple countries and time zones." A single follow-up cadence sent at US business hours hits India at midnight, East Africa at 3am, Southeast Asia at 2am. Non-response gets logged as inactivity and triggers hardware recovery — but it isn't churn, it's a timezone mismatch. Ops currently has no way to distinguish the two. The fix is a spreadsheet problem: segment response rates by geography before escalating. Right now, you're flagging engaged contributors as inactive and burning re-engagement budget on people who just got an email at the wrong hour.

Comms × Retention

JD: "Manage communications with contractors across multiple countries and time zones" — the gap is in how non-response is currently interpreted.

02
Hardware inactivity is a churn signal, not a logistics ticket

At 5,000+ contributors across 3 continents, some portion of shipped headbands sit in drawers — tracked as inventory items, not behavioral signals. Inactivity after day 7 reliably predicts permanent dropout. Catching it early means re-engagement, hardware recovery, or reallocation before the asset is gone. Right now "coordinate supply chain logistics" and "monitor contractor activity" are treated as two separate tasks. They're actually one connected signal: the hardware's location and activity status together tell you exactly who needs intervention and when.

Logistics × Retention

JD: "Coordinate supply chain logistics" + "Monitor contractor activity" — currently two tasks. They're one signal.

03
Time-to-first-recording is the activation metric nobody is watching

The JD says "onboard new contractors and guide them through setup." Between hardware shipped and first recording made, there's a gap — days or weeks of silence — that is the highest-dropout moment in any crowdsourced platform. Without tracking time-to-first-recording per cohort, per geography, per channel, ops is flying blind on its most important funnel metric. First recording = activation. Everything before it is a funnel. Right now it's probably just a "pending" row in a spreadsheet with no structured follow-up cadence tied to days elapsed.

Onboarding × Activation

JD: "Onboard new contractors and guide them through setup" — activation is the missing metric between setup and first recording.

04
B2B quality feedback is severed from contributors

When a robotics lab flags that 30% of a batch is unusable, that feedback goes to a founder's inbox. It never maps back to specific contributors. The people best positioned to define "quality" — your paying customers — have no structured way to improve the people producing the data. A simple intake system that maps customer complaints to contributor IDs, environments, and task types would let ops predict which contributor profiles produce usable data before deploying more hardware to similar profiles.

B2B → Contributor Loop

JD: "Track and organize data across spreadsheets and internal tools" — this is the exact function that closes this loop.

03 — The Plan

8 weeks. Four phases.
One shipped system.

Not a task list — a sequence where each phase depends on the last. By week 8, there's a feedback loop that didn't exist before I arrived.

Weeks 1–2
Audit
  • Map the full contributor lifecycle — signup to payment, every handoff
  • Document how non-response is currently tracked and escalated
  • Catalog all B2B quality complaint intake points (inbox, Slack, calls)
  • Identify time-to-first-recording for the last 3 cohorts — does this number even exist?
→ Lifecycle map with every gap annotated
Weeks 3–4
Diagnose
  • Build timezone-segmented response tracker — separate non-response by geography before flagging as churn
  • Flag hardware inactive at day 7, 14, 30 — build cohort dropout analysis
  • Track time-to-first-recording by geography and channel going forward
  • Map last 3 B2B quality complaints back to specific contributor IDs and environments
→ Churn cohort analysis + activation funnel baseline
Weeks 5–6
Build
  • Stand up contributor ops dashboard in Notion or Airtable — activity, hardware status, geography, activation date in one view
  • Create B2B feedback intake form that maps customer complaints to contributor IDs and environment types
  • Design timezone-aware communication schedules per region
  • Pilot with a cohort of 20–30 contributors across two geographies
→ Live dashboard + piloted feedback loop
Weeks 7–8
Ship
  • Roll out timezone-aware comms to full network
  • Measure: does segmented follow-up reduce false churn flags?
  • Present: which contributor profiles, geographies, and environments produce highest-quality data
  • Write the ops playbook — how to run this system without me
→ Shipped system + ops playbook + data on contributor quality profiles
